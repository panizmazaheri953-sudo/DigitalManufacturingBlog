01 — The Method
DES

What Is
Plant Simulation?

Discrete Event Simulation (DES)
A method of modelling a production system where each event is processed in sequence, parts move through stations, machines start and stop, queues build up. Tecnomatix simulates thousands of production cycles in seconds, revealing bottlenecks and capacity limits before any physical investment is made.
Siemens Tecnomatix Plant Simulation
Industry-standard simulation software used by manufacturers worldwide to validate production capacity, optimise layouts, and identify bottlenecks digitally. Used here to confirm the 25-printer Bambu P1S fleet can deliver 50,000 casing pairs per year within the defined schedule.
Why Simulation Before Production?
Buying 25 Bambu P1S printers (£11,750 capital) without first validating they can hit 50,000 units/year would be a significant financial risk. DES removes that uncertaintyand the simulation proves the fleet size, schedule, and layout before any physical commitment is made.
Build-Then-Validate Approach
Rather than modelling everything at once, the simulation was built incrementally meaning FESTO CP Lab first, then robot material handling, then the full printer fleet. Each stage was validated before the next was added, ensuring the model was accurate at every layer.
Why Tecnomatix specifically?
Tecnomatix Plant Simulation is part of the Siemens Digital Industries Software suite — the same ecosystem used by automotive, aerospace, and electronics manufacturers globally. It integrates directly with IoT platforms like Siemens Insights Hub, making it the natural choice for a project that includes real-time monitoring and digital twin functionality. The simulation outputs can be directly connected to live production data, turning a planning tool into an ongoing operational asset.

25 Printers Deployed
5 Rows × 5 Printers + Sorting + Broker.

A full additive manufacturing cell was deployed with 25 Bambu P1S printers arranged in 5 rows of 5 printers each. Each row includes a dedicated sorter where workers collect finished cases. A broker system manages job allocation, and a workstation handles prep tasks.A workplace was al.so added in station 3 for the manual task of festo CP Lab.

Printer Setup: 25 Bambu P1S (5 rows × 5 printers) — each printer prints top + bottom simultaneously = 1 full case (2 parts/print)
Prep Time: 30 minutes per shift for workers to prepare printers (cleaning, filament loading, calibration)
Sorter per Row: Finished cases collected by workers → placed on row sorter → aggregated → transported to FESTO CP Lab input
Broker System: Manages job distribution across all 25 printers, balances workload, and handles failed print reallocation
Workers: 2 workers per shift — handle prep (30 min), collect finished cases, operate sorters, transport to FESTO CP Lab
Real Output: 225 full cases printed in a full day (with 2 workers, including prep time, break, and transport)
Simulation Simplification: Each case = 1 unit (top+bottom combined) — reduces complexity when feeding into FESTO CP Lab

04 — Findings
OUT

What the Simulation
Proved.

73K
Annual capacity achieved
The 25 printer fleet running 24/7 weekdays plus 8 hour weekends produced 73,000 cases in 365 days. The 50,000 target was reached in just 253 days, leaving 112 days of buffer for unplanned downtime, maintenance, and the 20 percent failure rate .
25
Optimal printer fleet size
Simulation confirmed 25 is the right number. Fewer printers miss the target. More printers add capital cost without proportional output gain. This finding aligns with established discrete event simulation methodology.
✓
80 percent yield absorbed
The 20 percent print failure rate was built into the simulation. The system still hits 50,000 because 62,500 print attempts per year produce 50,000 good pairs. The failure rate is planned for, not an exception .
3×
Capital saved versus single shift
A single shift scenario would require 75 printers to hit 50,000 per year, three times the fleet and capital cost. Running 25 printers 24/7 is the financially optimal configuration, confirmed by simulation.
2
Optimal workers per shift
After running the experiment manager, 2 workers per shift was confirmed as optimal. Adding more workers (3 or 4 per shift) produced no measurable increase in output. The printers themselves are the real bottleneck, not the operators .
S5
CP Lab bottleneck is the oven
The oven at Station 5 runs at 100 percent utilisation, making it the CP Lab bottleneck. Pick by Light runs at approximately 70 percent. The printers remain the overall system bottleneck, not Station 5.
✓
3 shift pattern validated
The morning, afternoon, and night shift pattern worked perfectly in simulation. Handover between shifts was seamless with no production gaps or queue buildup. This validates the shift structure for 24/7 operation.
✓
Target met with safety buffer
Fifty thousand cases achieved in 253 days, not 365. The extra 112 days provide real operational margin for unplanned maintenance, recalibration, and the 20 percent failure rate. The design exceeds the requirement with significant safety buffer .
