01 — The Brief
01
Paniz Manufacturing Co.
Manchester, UK

The Phones-R-Us
Challenge.

Paniz Manufacturing was commissioned by Phones-R-Us to produce 50,000 interchangeable phone casings per year. The client provided a 2D engineering drawing for the bottom casing and a physical reference part for the top — both had to be manufactured to tolerance, assembled through a live FESTO CP Lab line, and proven production-viable at annual scale.

This wasn't just a prototyping exercise. The brief demanded a complete digital manufacturing system — from initial CAD through to capacity simulation, process failure analysis, quality control with statistical evidence, and a working business case. Every decision had to be justified with data. Every failure mode had to be anticipated and controlled.

The golden rule: It is all about the process — not the product. This portfolio exists to showcase problem-solving capability, engineering judgement, and hands-on experience with the tools that define modern digital manufacturing. The phone casing is the vehicle. The thinking behind it is what matters.
PROCESS OVER PRODUCT
In Industry 4.0, the main driver is data. The more information you have about a manufacturing system, the easier it is to implement, optimise, and scale. This project was designed to simulate the full lifecycle of a real manufacturing company — from brief to business model — using the same tools, frameworks, and constraints you'd face on the factory floor.
02 — Process Journey

9-Week
Journey.

W1
Phase 01 — Design Input
Client Brief & 2D Drawing Analysis
The project began with the Phones-R-Us client brief and a 2D technical drawing of the bottom casing. Before any CAD work, this drawing was dissected — every dimension, tolerance, and feature catalogued. The top casing had no drawing: it required physical reverse engineering from the client's reference part, using manual measurement and digital calipers to extract every geometry needed for a faithful CAD model.
Client Drawing Reviewed Reverse Engineering Initiated Dimensional Audit Complete
W2
Phase 02 — CAD Modelling
Fusion 360 — Top & Bottom Casing
Both casings were modelled in Autodesk Fusion 360. The bottom casing was built directly from the technical drawing — every dimension, boss, and port replicated precisely. The top casing was reverse-engineered: a 3D model constructed from physical measurements, cross-referenced against the reference part until every surface matched. Particular attention was paid to the mating features between top and bottom — the press-fit geometry that would later be tested at FESTO Station 4.
Bottom Casing — From Drawing Top Casing — Reverse Engineered Press-Fit Features Modelled
W3–4
Phase 03 — Initial Print Trials
Ultimaker S3/S5 — First Attempt
With CAD complete, the first prints were run on the Ultimaker S3 and S5 Extended. Fifteen prints were produced. The results were deeply unsatisfactory — 10 prints failed outright, and of the 7 that looked visually acceptable, not a single one passed the FESTO laser measurement station. The critical failure: wall depth measuring 1.18mm against a required 1.6–2.2mm. Quality was inconsistent, layer shifting was frequent, and support removal was damaging parts. The Ultimaker platform was clearly incapable of holding the dimensional tolerances this job required. A decision was made: change the printer, not the design.
15 Prints — 10 Failures 0 / 7 Pass FESTO Laser Decision: Switch Platform
W4–5
Phase 04 — Platform Switch
Bambu Lab P1S — The Right Tool
The switch to the Bambu Lab P1S was transformative. Five test prints were run. Four were successful — three of those were dimensionally perfect across all FESTO stations. The one failure was a USB interruption mid-print (not a quality issue — eliminated immediately by switching to cloud printing). Wall thickness achieved: 1.9mm, comfortably within specification. The Bambu's closed-chamber environment, AMS auto-calibration, and 500mm/s print speed produced the dimensional consistency the Ultimaker never could. Settings were locked, the slicer profile saved, and the platform selected for full production.
4 / 5 Successful Prints 1.9mm Wall Thickness ✓ Cloud Printing Enabled Production Settings Locked
W3–5
Phase 05 — Risk Analysis
PFMEA — Failure Before It Happens
Running concurrently with print trials, the Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis was built out across all four process areas: 3D printing (both platforms), FESTO assembly, and 24/7 capacity operation. Twelve failure modes were identified. The highest RPN was 400 — an operator safety risk from sharp casing edges, addressed immediately with chamfered edges and a mandatory deburring station. The Ultimaker's dimensional failure (RPN 288) was already being resolved by the platform switch. PFMEA wasn't retrospective documentation — it was used to drive decisions in real time.
12 Failure Modes Logged Max RPN 400 → 80 Safety Items Actioned
W6
Phase 06 — Traceability
Part Identification & Traceability System
Every casing produced was given a unique part identifier — embedded directly into the print or applied as a label — to enable full manufacturing traceability. This included batch number, printer ID, print date, and slicer settings version. In a 50,000-unit annual production run, traceability isn't optional: it's the mechanism by which defects are traced back to source, SPC data is meaningful, and recalls are manageable. The system was designed to integrate with the broader Siemens Insights Hub IIoT platform as the production line scales.
Part IDs Embedded Batch Tracking Active IIoT Integration Planned
W6–7
Phase 07 — Quality Control
SPC Analysis & Machine Qualification
With the Bambu P1S confirmed as the production printer, formal Statistical Process Control was implemented. Wall thickness and key dimensional measurements were tracked across multiple prints, control charts generated, and the process capability confirmed. The Bambu achieved ±0.1mm dimensional tolerance against a maximum allowable of ±0.15mm — process capability confirmed. Machine qualification was completed: the Bambu P1S fleet was officially certified as production-ready. The Ultimaker was formally retired from the process.
SPC Charts Generated ±0.1mm Achieved Bambu P1S Qualified Ultimaker Retired
W7–8
Phase 08 — Simulation
Siemens Tecnomatix — Plant Simulation
The production system was modelled in Siemens Tecnomatix Plant Simulation using Discrete Event Simulation. The full 25-printer Bambu fleet was simulated under 24/7 operating conditions, validating the capacity model against the 50,000-unit annual target. Key outputs included value stream mapping, takt time analysis, process flow diagrams, and a manufacturing sequence chart. Bottleneck analysis identified the FESTO Station 2 laser measurement as the rate-limiting step under high throughput — leading to the recommendation for a secondary laser station in the full production build. The Siemens Insights Hub IIoT platform was explored for real-time machine data integration.
DES Model Built 50K/Year Validated Bottleneck Identified Value Stream Mapped
W9
Phase 09 — Delivery
Prototype Submission & Full Report
Week 9 was delivery. The prototype casings — printed on the Bambu P1S, dimensionally verified, and fully traced — were submitted alongside a complete digital manufacturing portfolio: PFMEA register, SPC data, capacity simulation, business model canvas, and this project website. The FESTO CP Lab run was completed, all 6 stations tested, and the assembly process formally verified. The minimum viable product had been built, measured, and learned from — through three distinct build-measure-learn cycles over the 9 weeks.
Prototype Delivered FESTO Run Complete Full Portfolio Submitted
03 — Methodology

Digital Mfg.
Toolkit.

Every methodology applied in this project maps to a real Industry 4.0 framework. These aren't academic exercises — they are the tools used to design, de-risk, simulate, and scale a production system from a blank sheet of paper to 50,000 units per year.

Lean Manufacturing
Value Stream Mapping
Every step in the production process — from raw filament to shipped casing — was mapped to identify value-adding vs non-value-adding activities. VSM exposed the Ultimaker changeover as pure waste, reinforcing the decision to standardise on the Bambu P1S fleet.
VSM
Production Planning
Takt Time Analysis
With a target of 50,000 casings per year and a 2.5-hour cycle time per set, takt time analysis validated the 25-printer 24/7 configuration. It also revealed that a single 8-hour shift would require 75 printers — a capital cost 3× higher for the same output.
TAKT
Process Design
Process Flow Diagram
A structured process flow chart documented every operation from design to dispatch — CAD, slicing, printing, quality inspection, FESTO assembly, and packing. This became the input data for the Siemens Tecnomatix simulation and the PFMEA process function column.
PFD
Quality Engineering
Statistical Process Control
Wall thickness and dimensional measurements from the Bambu P1S fleet were tracked on control charts. SPC confirmed the process was in control (±0.1mm vs ±0.15mm spec), machine capability was calculated, and the production baseline was locked before scaling to 25 printers.
SPC
Risk Management
PFMEA Analysis
Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis was applied across all four process areas. Twelve failure modes were identified and scored. Every high-RPN item had a corrective action assigned, a responsible owner, a target date, and a verified post-action RPN — turning risk analysis into a live control document.
PFMEA
Simulation
Discrete Event Simulation
Tecnomatix Plant Simulation was used to model the production system as a series of events — each print cycle, quality check, and FESTO station modelled as a discrete operation with its own process time, failure rate, and resource constraint. The model validated the 50K/year target and pinpointed Station 2 as the throughput bottleneck.
DES
Industry 4.0
IIoT — Siemens Insights Hub
The Industrial Internet of Things layer was explored through the Siemens Insights Hub platform. In a scaled production environment, real-time machine data from the Bambu P1S fleet — temperatures, print progress, failure alerts — feeds directly into a cloud dashboard, enabling predictive maintenance and eliminating unplanned downtime.
IIoT
Strategy
Business Model Canvas
The business model canvas framed Paniz Manufacturing as a commercial entity — value proposition, customer segments, key resources, cost structure, and revenue streams all mapped out. This grounded the technical manufacturing work in a commercial reality: we aren't just printing parts, we are running a manufacturing business.
BMC
Validation
Machine Qualification & Process Capability
Before committing to 25 printers, the Bambu P1S was formally qualified: process capability indices calculated, gauge repeatability confirmed, and a controlled acceptance test run. Qualification is what separates a promising prototype from a production-ready system. The Ultimaker failed this step; the Bambu passed it.

Every build was measured against hard criteria — FESTO laser station pass/fail, wall thickness, dimensional accuracy. Not gut feel. Data. The laser measurement station made the Ultimaker's failure undeniable. SPC made the Bambu's success provable.

06 — Quality System
Traceability
& IIoT
Every casing carries a unique identifier — batch number, printer ID, print date, slicer version. In a 50,000-unit annual run, traceability is the backbone of a quality system: it's what makes SPC data actionable, defect root-cause analysis possible, and a recall manageable without scrapping an entire production batch.
Unique part ID per casing — embedded or labelled
Batch number linked to printer, date, and slicer profile version
FESTO station pass/fail logged against each part ID

IIoT integration with Siemens Insights Hub planned for full-scale
