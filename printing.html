Why Ultimaker Failed
Root Cause 1
Open frame = temperature gradients. Without an enclosure, ambient air cooled different parts of the print unevenly, causing layer warping and inconsistent extrusion which directly resulting in the 1.18mm wall measurement.
Root Cause 2
Bowden tube extruder. The longer filament path between motor and nozzle reduces retraction precision and introduces pressure lag, causing inconsistent extrusion particularly in thin-wall sections.
Root Cause 3
Hardware reliability. Multiple mid-print failures due to filament sensor errors, nozzle clogs, and calibration drift across ~20 attempts. Unacceptable for a 50,000/year production commitment.
Root Cause 4
Insufficient brim size. A wider brim helps the print stay attached to the bed and prevents warping and more importantly, it can prevent nozzle clogging, which is difficult to repair on the Ultimaker. This setting was not optimised in early PrintCity trials.
Mid Print Failure
Unreliable filament detection. The Ultimaker's filament sensor triggered false positives multiple times, stopping prints mid way for no reason. Each stop wasted 1 to 2 hours of print time and 50g to 100g of PLA. Restarting required manual intervention and recalibration.
Mid Print Failure
Partial and full clogs stopped multiple prints. The Bowden tube extruder is prone to pressure buildup, leading to clogs. Clearing a clog required a cold pull procedure, recalibration of the extruder, and often a complete print restart. This alone caused the loss of several casings.
Poor Final Print Quality
Corners lifted from the print bed. Without an enclosure, the bottom layers cooled faster than the upper layers. This thermal gradient caused the corners of the phone casing to curl upward, creating a warped final part that could not be used in assembly.
Dimensional Inaccuracy
1.18mm measured vs 1.6mm CAD minimum. The CAD model specified a difference measurement of 2mm. The Ultimaker consistently printed walls at wrong dimensions like 1.18 which is 26 percent below the design minimum. This was not a CAD error; the same file printed correctly on the Bambu P1S at 1.9mm.
Pre Print Setup including Bed Leveling and Filament Changes
Manual bed leveling was slow and unreliable. Before every print, the Ultimaker required manual bed leveling using a piece of paper to set the nozzle height. This process was imprecise and often took 15 to 20 minutes. If the leveling was slightly off, the first layer would fail and the whole process had to restart. Changing filament was also a hassle; unloading the old PLA required heating the nozzle, manually feeding the new filament, and waiting for the extruder to purge properly. Each filament change added another 10 to 15 minutes of setup time. Combined, these pre print steps meant every Ultimaker print took 1 to 2 hours just to get the printer ready before the actual print could even start. For a production environment aiming at 50,000 units per year, this manual overhead was completely unacceptable.
Why Bambu P1S Solved It
Solution 1: Fully Enclosed Chamber
Stable thermal environment. The fully enclosed chamber significantly reduces temperature gradients and prevents the uneven cooling that caused warping and delamination on the Ultimaker. This is essential for consistent thin wall printing.
Solution 2: Direct Drive Extruder
Precise filament control. The filament motor sits directly above the nozzle, providing precise retraction and consistent extrusion pressure. This eliminated the pressure lag and inconsistent extrusion that plagued the Ultimaker Bowden system, achieving a 1.9mm difference measurement on the first successful print.
Solution 3: High Speed and Reliability
500mm/s capability with cloud monitoring. The Bambu P1S can print at up to 500mm/s, reducing the print cycle for both casings to 2.5 hours. Bambu Cloud enables remote monitoring and alerts, and the auto calibration system dramatically improves hardware stability, enabling 24/7 unattended operation.
Solution 4: Automatic Bed Leveling
Fast and precise leveling. The Bambu P1S uses a load cell sensor to automatically level the bed before every print. The process takes approximately 2 minutes and requires no manual adjustment. First layer adhesion is consistently excellent, eliminating the unreliable paper based leveling of the Ultimaker.
Solution 5: Reliable Filament System
Accurate filament detection. The filament sensor in the Bambu P1S is generally reliable. For production environments, the optional AMS (Automatic Material System) provides automatic filament changes and runout handling without operator intervention. Together these features significantly reduce mid print failures.
Solution 6: Nozzle Clog Prevention
Direct drive reduces clogs. The direct drive extruder eliminates the pressure buildup that causes clogs in Bowden systems. For abrasive filaments, a hardened steel nozzle is recommended. Clearing a clog when it does occur is also simpler due to the accessible extruder design.
Solution 7: Dimensional Accuracy
Consistent and predictable shrinkage. The enclosed chamber maintains a stable temperature throughout the print, so shrinkage is consistent from part to part. With proper calibration and suitable filament settings, the Bambu P1S can achieve dimensional accuracy within ±0.1mm. The same CAD file that measured 1.18mm on the Ultimaker measured 1.9mm on the Bambu.
Solution 8: Warping Prevention
Enclosure plus heated bed. The Bambu P1S keeps the entire print at a consistent elevated temperature, with the bed at 60°C for PLA and the chamber passively reaching 40 to 50°C. This eliminates the temperature gradient that causes corners to lift. Combined with the built in brim settings, warping is almost completely eliminated.
Solution 9: Fast Setup and Filament Changes
Automated loading and unloading. The Bambu P1S has a built in filament loading and unloading routine. Select Load on the screen, insert filament, and the printer heats the nozzle and feeds the filament automatically. The AMS system makes filament changes even faster. Total filament change time is approximately 2 minutes, compared to 10 to 15 minutes on the Ultimaker.

Across approximately 20 Ultimaker attempts, multiple prints stopped mid way through. Causes included filament sensor triggers, layer adhesion failures causing the part to detach from the bed, and calibration drift between print jobs. Each failure meant wasted 1 to 2 hours of print time and material loss.
Root Causes of Mid Print Stops
Filament sensor errors. The Ultimaker's filament sensor triggered false positives multiple times, stopping prints for no reason. Each stop required manual intervention and recalibration.

Partial and full nozzle clogs. The Bowden tube extruder is prone to pressure buildup, leading to clogs. Clearing a clog required a cold pull procedure and often a complete print restart.

Poor bed adhesion. The part detached from the build plate during printing. Without the part staying firmly attached, the printer continued moving but the part shifted, causing layer shifting or complete detachment.

Dirty or greasy build plate. Oils from fingers or old glue residue reduced adhesion. Cleaning the plate with isopropyl alcohol was essential before every print, adding another 5 minutes to setup time.

Layer height issues. Using a layer height too large for the nozzle diameter reduced inter layer bonding. The Ultimaker struggled with consistent extrusion at finer layer heights due to Bowden tube pressure lag.

Around 10 complete casing pairs printed successfully on the Ultimaker (These 5 are visual examples) but every single pair failed either visual inspection, FESTO Station 2 measurement, or both. Some looked acceptable but measured wrong;y. Others showed warping, surface defects, or layer shifting that made them unsuitable for client delivery. Not a single pair passed both tests.
Why These Pairs Failed: Visual & Measurement
Warping from temperature gradients. Without an enclosure, the bottom layers cooled faster than the upper layers. This thermal gradient caused the corners of the phone casings to curl upward, creating warped parts that could not be submitted.

difference measurement below CAD specification. The Ultimaker consistently printed walls at 1.18mm instead of the required 1.6 to 2.2mm range. This was not visible to the eye and the parts looked acceptable but failed FESTO laser measurement at Station 2.

Layer shifting from stepper motor skips. The open frame amplifies vibration. Loose belts or excessive speed caused the print head to lose position, resulting in visible horizontal shifts across the casing walls.

Poor inter layer adhesion and delamination. Layers cooled too fast in the open air environment before the next layer could bond properly. This created weak bonds that separated under light mechanical force.

Surface defects from inconsistent extrusion. The Bowden tube extruder created pressure lag, causing uneven flow and rough surface finish on the casing exterior.

Incorrect slicing settings. Wrong retraction settings caused stringing and blobs. Z hop settings that were too low caused the nozzle to drag through the print, scarring the top surface.
Why the Bambu P1S Fixed These Problems
The Bambu P1S eliminates all of these issues. The first successful Bambu print achieved a difference measurement of 1.9mm: directly in the middle of the specification: and passed FESTO Station 2 immediately. The enclosed chamber prevents warping, the direct drive extruder provides consistent extrusion, and the surface finish is smooth and professional.

This was the decisive failure. Several Ultimaker prints looked visually acceptable had smooth surface, correct shape. But every single one failed at FESTO Station 2 laser measurement. The wall was consistently out of specification. The part looked right but measured wrong.
Root Causes of Dimensional Inaccuracy
difference measurement deviation from CAD. The CAD model specified a difference measurementof 2mm. The Ultimaker consistently printed walls wrong. This was not a CAD error but error on the setting and layering and changing the layering ended up causing other failures in printing; the same file printed correctly on the Bambu P1S at 1.9mm.

Partial nozzle clog and under extrusion. Small debris or heat creep built up inside the nozzle. Flow became inconsistent. Walls printed thinner than specified because the reduced flow rate meant less filament was being deposited.

Temperature fluctuations. The open frame design allowed cold drafts to hit the print. Uneven thermal contraction meant different parts of the same print shrank by different amounts, distorting critical dimensions.

These casings printed fully on the Ultimaker: they did not stop mid print. However, every single one failed either visual inspection, FESTO Station 2 measurement, or both. The top and bottom casings shown here represent the best results the Ultimaker could achieve, yet none were acceptable for production. Before any of these prints could even start, the Ultimaker required 1 to 2 hours of manual setup time.
Root Causes: Pre Print Setup Overhead
Manual bed leveling was slow and unreliable. Before every print, the Ultimaker required manual bed leveling using a piece of paper to set the nozzle height. This process was imprecise and often took 15 to 20 minutes. If the leveling was slightly off, the first layer would fail and the whole process had to restart.

Filament changes were a hassle. Unloading the old PLA required heating the nozzle, manually feeding the new filament, and waiting for the extruder to purge properly. Each filament change added another 10 to 15 minutes of setup time.

Insufficient brim size. A wider brim helps the print stay attached to the bed and prevents warping. More importantly, it can prevent nozzle clogging by maintaining consistent filament flow. This setting was not optimised in early PrintCity trials.

Poor support settings: bottom cases failed on the back. Several bottom casings failed because the back surface required supports that were either not enabled or incorrectly configured in the slicer. Without proper supports, overhangs sagged and created rough, unusable surfaces. After switching to the Bambu P1S, support settings were optimised and the back surfaces printed cleanly.

QR code printed directly on the casing failed. Initial attempts to print the QR code directly onto the phone casing using the Ultimaker failed consistently. The small details of the QR code could not be resolved reliably, resulting in unreadable codes. This is why ENCLOS3D switched to using adhesive QR stickers instead: a more reliable solution for traceability at Station 1.

Combined pre print overhead. Combined, these pre print steps meant every Ultimaker print took 1 to 2 hours just to get the printer ready before the actual print could even start. For a production environment aiming at 50,000 units per year, this manual overhead was completely unacceptable.

Even successful prints failed quality checks. As shown in the photos above, even when the Ultimaker successfully completed a print without stopping, the results were still rejected due to warping, surface defects, or difference measurement being out of specification (1.18mm instead of 1.6 to 2.2mm).
Why the Bambu P1S Fixed These Problems
The Bambu P1S eliminates the pre print overhead entirely. Automatic bed leveling takes 2 minutes with no paper or guesswork. Filament changes are automated via the AMS system or a simple one button routine. Support settings in Bambu Studio produce clean overhangs, eliminating the back surface issues seen on the Ultimaker bottom casings. QR codes are now applied as adhesive stickers at Station 1, bypassing the unreliable direct printing method. The enclosed chamber and direct drive extruder produce consistent, high quality prints that pass FESTO Station 2 on the first attempt: no 1 to 2 hour setup required.
