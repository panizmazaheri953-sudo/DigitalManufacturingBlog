What is PFMEA?

What is PFMEA?

Process Failure Mode and Effects Analysis (PFMEA) is a systematic method for identifying potential failures in a manufacturing process and prioritising actions to prevent them. For the Phones-R-Us project, PFMEA was used to analyse the 3D printing process and FESTO CP Lab assembly line.

The main goals of FMEA are:

  • Identify critical components to ensure quality of production
  • Identify possible failures early to ensure production functions
  • Estimate risks before they occur
  • Reduce or remove cost of changes after mass production starts
  • Support continuous improvement

The Risk Priority Number (RPN) is calculated as: RPN = Severity × Occurrence × Detection

This PFMEA is based on actual manufacturing data from the Phones-R-Us project, comparing two different 3D printing platforms: Ultimaker S3/S5 Extended (initial trials) and Bambu Lab P1S (final production).

Production Capacity Analysis

Target: 50,000 complete phones per year (50,000 top casings + 50,000 bottom casings)

Print time (Bambu P1S): 2.5 hours for BOTH casings together (1 complete phone set)

Actual success rate: 80% (1 in 5 fails, based on 4 of 5 successful prints)

Working days: 260 days/year (Monday-Friday, 52 weeks)

Total cases needed per day (80% success rate): 231

Actual Trial Results vs PFMEA Predictions

Ultimaker S3/S5 Extended (15 prints): 10 failed prints (66% failure rate). 7 prints looked acceptable visually but 2 passed FESTO measurement station. Primary failure: Depth measurement reading 1.18mm (spec required 1.6-2.2mm).

Bambu Lab P1S (5 prints): 4 successful prints (80% success rate). 3 prints "perfect" - passed all FESTO stations. 1 failure due to USB interruption.

Key Insight: The CAD design was correct. Switching from Ultimaker to Bambu P1S reduced RPN from 288 to 64.

Reflection & Innovation

Printer Selection as a Critical Control Point: Traditional FMEA frameworks often assume that process capability is fixed and focus on mitigating failures through detection and control mechanisms. However, this project demonstrated that equipment selection itself is a critical upstream control variable that directly influences risk. The Ultimaker platform consistently failed to meet dimensional requirements (RPN 288), whereas switching to the Bambu P1S reduced occurrence significantly (RPN 64). This indicates that rather than relying solely on downstream inspection, redesigning the process through improved equipment selection can fundamentally lower risk at its source — effectively reducing Occurrence rather than compensating through Detection.

Economic and Operational Analysis of Production: From a production systems perspective, achieving 50,000 units/year can be approached either by increasing asset quantity (75+ printers operating on 8-hour shifts) or by maximising asset utilisation (25 printers operating continuously). The model is significantly more efficient because it increases utilisation from approximately 33% (8 hours out of 24) to near 100%. This reduces the required number of machines by two-thirds, leading to substantial capital savings (estimated £50,000+), as well as lower costs associated with floor space, infrastructure, maintenance, and operator labour.

Additionally, fewer machines improve process consistency and control. With 75+ printers, variability between machines (calibration drift, wear, environmental differences) increases process variation and raises Occurrence risk. In contrast, a smaller, continuously operating fleet allows tighter control, standardisation, and easier implementation of Statistical Process Control (SPC). This improves overall process capability (Cp/Cpk) and reduces the likelihood of defects at scale. Therefore, the strategy is not only economically advantageous but also operationally more robust.

FMEA Trade-Off: Occurrence vs Detection in Unattended Operation: While operation reduces Occurrence through improved process control and equipment efficiency, it introduces a critical trade-off by increasing Detection risk. Unattended operation means failures such as print interruptions, material defects, or thermal issues may not be immediately identified, potentially increasing waste or downtime. In FMEA terms, this shifts risk from Occurrence to Detection.

To balance this trade-off, additional control measures must be implemented. Remote monitoring systems, cloud-based alerts, live camera feeds, and automated shutdown mechanisms act as enhanced detection controls, effectively lowering the Detection rating. This demonstrates a key FMEA principle: risk can be redistributed across Severity, Occurrence, and Detection, but must ultimately be managed holistically. In this case, technological controls compensate for the lack of human supervision.

Safety as a Critical Constraint: Continuous, unattended operation introduces significant safety considerations, particularly the risk of thermal runaway or electrical faults in additive manufacturing systems. Unlike traditional shift-based production, failures occurring overnight may escalate without immediate intervention. Therefore, safety must be treated as a primary design constraint rather than a secondary consideration. The implementation of fire suppression systems (e.g. fireball extinguishers), thermal protection, certified equipment, and environmental monitoring is essential. These measures reduce Severity and Detection risks, ensuring that operational efficiency does not compromise safety.

Conclusion: Switching to Bambu P1S reduced Occurrence from 9 to 2, lowering RPN from 288 to 64. Adopting a production strategy with 25 printers provides the optimal balance between capital efficiency, process stability, and scalability. However, this approach is only viable when supported by robust monitoring and safety systems that mitigate the increased Detection risks associated with unattended operation. This project demonstrates that effective risk reduction is achieved not only through analysis, but through strategic process and system design decisions.
