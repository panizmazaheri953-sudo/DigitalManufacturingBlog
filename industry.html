The Manufacturing
Landscape.

Consumer electronics manufacturing sits at the intersection of relentless cost pressure from global supply chains and increasing demand for customisation from end consumers. For decades, the answer to both was injection moulding at massive scale. But that answer is being disrupted.

The global smartphone market shipped 1.24 billion units in 2023 (IDC, 2024). Behind every unit is a casing — and the casing manufacturing decision is more nuanced than it appears. At volumes of tens of millions, injection moulding dominates. But at 50,000 units per year — the Phones-R-Us brief — the calculus changes fundamentally.

The Volume Threshold: Injection moulding tooling costs typically range from £10,000 to £100,000 per mould, making it economically viable only above roughly 100,000–500,000 units (Wohlers & Gornet, 2020). At 50,000 units/year, the economics flip — additive manufacturing becomes the smarter choice.

Why not injection moulding? At 50,000 units/year, tooling cost alone — conservatively £25,000–£50,000 — represents 50–100p per unit before material. Christensen's disruption theory (1997) predicts this: incumbent technologies are over-engineered for small-batch, fast-iteration requirements.

Wohlers and Gornet (2020) establish that the crossover point — where injection moulding becomes cost-competitive with additive manufacturing — sits between 100,000 and 500,000 units. At 50,000, we are decisively on the additive side of that line.

04 — Theory
DI
Christensen's Framework

Disruptive
Innovation.

Clayton Christensen's theory of disruptive innovation — from The Innovator's Dilemma (1997) — describes how new technologies enter markets at the low end before improving until they displace the mainstream. 3D printing is following this arc exactly.

📸 Christensen's disruption S-curve — incumbent vs disruptive technology trajectories.
Figure 4 Disruptive innovation trajectory — 3D printing following Christensen's classic pattern

Rayna and Striukova (2021) apply Christensen's framework specifically to additive manufacturing, arguing that 3D printing disrupts not just manufacturing processes but the entire business model of physical production — collapsing design, prototyping, and manufacturing into a single digital-physical workflow.

The Lean Start-Up Connection: Eric Ries' Build → Measure → Learn cycle (2011) — applied throughout this project — democratises iteration. In manufacturing, the MVP is the first print. The measurement is the FESTO station. The learning is the pivot from Ultimaker to Bambu.
