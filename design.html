Top Casing
Design Process

The top casing presented the most significant engineering challenge of this project. With no complete engineering drawing provided, the entire model had to be built from physical measurements taken from the real component using callipers and cross-referencing multiple angles.

This process — known as reverse engineering — required a deep understanding of how Fusion 360 handles parametric feature construction. Each sketch was built layer by layer, with unknown dimensions derived mathematically from known proportional relationships.

The most challenging features were the internal lip geometry, the camera aperture positioning, and the subtle draft angles on the side walls — none of which were explicitly documented, and all of which were critical to achieving a press-fit assembly later in the process.

Engineering Challenges Overcome
  • Missing cross-sectional dimensions — derived from proportion analysis
  • Unclear feature positions — reconstructed via relative distance mapping
  • Complex curved surfaces — modelled using incremental feature construction
  • Internal lip geometry — critical for press-fit at Station 2 (FESTO)
  • Camera cutout and button aperture alignment across all views
Fusion 360 Methodology
Step 1 — Physical measurement: Callipers used to record all accessible external dimensions. Sketches produced at 1:1 scale.
Step 2 — 2D drawing construction: Multi-view orthographic drawing built in Fusion 360. All ambiguous dimensions flagged and resolved.
Step 3 — 3D parametric model: Solid body extruded from base sketch. Complex features added incrementally. Accuracy validated at every stage.
Step 4 — STL export: High-resolution mesh exported. Geometry integrity verified in slicer before handoff to 3D printing stage.

Bottom Casing
Design Process

The bottom casing was modelled directly from a complete client engineering drawing — a significantly more structured workflow than the reverse-engineered top casing. Every dimension, datum, and feature relationship was clearly defined from the outset.

The primary engineering objective was precision translation: ensuring that every dimension in the 2D drawing was faithfully reproduced in the 3D model without accumulated tolerancing errors. Fusion 360's parametric constraint system was critical to maintaining feature relationships throughout the build.

The bottom casing is 1.3mm deeper than the top, which has direct implications for the press-fit assembly at Station 2 of the FESTO CP Lab testing rig. Maintaining the depth specification within ±0.15mm was treated as a critical control point from the very first sketch.

Key Engineering Decisions
  • All features constructed from fully constrained parametric sketches
  • Datum references aligned to match client drawing coordinate system
  • Depth tolerance treated as critical — directly affects assembly fit
  • USB port recess and speaker grille modelled to exact drawing specifications
  • Regular cross-checks between Fusion 360 model and original drawing throughout
Fusion 360 Methodology
Step 1 — Drawing interpretation: Client engineering drawing studied in full. Datums, tolerances, and feature hierarchies identified before opening Fusion 360.
Step 2 — Fully constrained sketch: Base profile built with all dimensions locked to drawing values. No derived or estimated inputs — all values directly entered.
Step 3 — Feature build: Shell, ports, and structural ribs added in sequence. Each feature verified against drawing before proceeding to the next.
Step 4 — STL export: High-resolution mesh at 0.001mm surface deviation. Geometry verified clean before handoff to 3D printing stage.
